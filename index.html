Recycle

Recycling right is critical for benefiting the environment and the economy. The #1 tip for recycling correctly is to make sure the items you put in the recycling bin are empty, clean and dry.

PLASTIC:

  • Recycle plastic by shape – bottles, tubs, jugs and cups
  • Lids with a diameter of at least 3 inches
  • Plant pots (no soil)
  • Note: labels on containers are okay

PAPER:

  • Newspapers plus inserts, magazines, mail, envelopes (including with windows)
  • Cardboard boxes
  • Wrapping paper (no foil)
  • Paperback books (if unable to donate)
  • Juice boxes, milk and ice cream cartons, broth containers
  • Paper cups, including coffee cups

METAL:

  • Soda/tin cans
  • Metal food cans
  • Clean aluminum foil and foil trays

GLASS:

  • Jars and bottles, any color
  • NO broken glass – safely wrap sharp edges and throw away

Garbage

King County has set the goal to achieve Zero Waste of Resources (ZWR) by 2030. You can help by becoming an "EcoConsumer" and being mindful of the impact of what you buy.

PLASTIC:

  • Preformed and clamshell plastic packaging
  • Caps and lids less than 3 inches in diameter (unless secured onto container)
  • Stretch wrap
  • Empty prescription bottles
  • Plastic trays, plates and utensils
  • Motor oil and antifreeze containers
  • Plastic bags – these can be recycled at many grocery stores

PAPER:

  • Foil wrapping paper and ribbon
  • Wax and plastic coated cardboard
  • Used tissues and paper towels with cleaning solutions or chemicals

OTHER:

  • Mirrors, window glass, broken glass
  • Broken ceramics and dishes
  • Empty aerosol spray cans
  • Sharp or greasy metal
  • Oil containers

Common Recycling Myths

Grow your recycling IQ by debunking common myths that can help ensure your materials aren’t contaminated and successfully make it all the way through the recycling process and to their next life. Learn the reality behind the myths to ensure you're protecting others and the environment by recycling right.

Reality: The recycling symbol alone does not guarantee that an item can be recycled. Different materials are accepted in different recycling programs, depending on local recycling capabilities.
Solution: Always check local recycling guidelines to know what materials are accepted. Many communities have specific rules for plastics, glass, and other materials, so it’s important to follow those instructions to prevent contamination.

Reality: Dirty containers can contaminate entire batches of recyclables, making them unusable.
Solution: Always rinse containers (such as food cans, plastic containers, and bottles) before recycling. This ensures that the items are clean and don’t spoil other recyclables.

Reality: Most curbside recycling programs don’t accept plastic bags because they can jam the machinery at recycling facilities.
Solution: Instead of placing plastic bags in the recycling bin, take them to designated collection bins often found at grocery stores or retailers.

Reality: Grease and food residue on pizza boxes can make them unrecyclable. Once contaminated, cardboard loses its recyclable quality.
Solution: Tear off the clean, non-greasy parts of the pizza box to recycle them, and dispose of the greasy part in the trash or compost if possible.

Reality: Not all plastics are created equal. Plastics are numbered 1 to 7, and only certain numbers (usually 1 and 2) are accepted in most recycling programs.
Solution: Check the number inside the recycling symbol to see if it’s accepted by your local program. Avoid purchasing non-recyclable plastics, and opt for reusable options when possible.
